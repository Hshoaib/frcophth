1 · The electromagnetic spectrum & optical radiation

Light is energy the human eye can detect. The deeper physical nature of light is still not fully understood, but its behaviour is mapped in detail — and that behaviour is the whole of clinical optics. Light is one slice of the electromagnetic spectrumThe full range of electromagnetic radiation, ordered by wavelengthThe length of one complete wave — the distance from one crest to the next. Symbol λ (lambda). Short λ = high energy; long λ = low energy.. From shortest to longest: cosmic rays → gamma rays → X-rays → ultraviolet → visible light → infrared → microwaves → radar → TV → radio. All travel at the same speed in a vacuum; they differ only in wavelength (and therefore frequency and energy)..

Optical radiation is the band that sits between X-rays and microwaves. It is divided into seven wavebands, grouped because wavelengths within each one provoke similar biological effects:

WavebandWavelength
UV-C200–280 nm
UV-B280–315 nm
UV-A315–400 nm
Visible400–780 nm
IR-A (near IR)780–1400 nm
IR-B1400–3000 nm
IR-C3000–10 000 nm

A nanometrenm = one-billionth of a metre (10⁻⁹ m). Visible light spans roughly 400 nm (violet) to 780 nm (red). is the natural unit here. The general rule: the shorter the wavelength, the more energy per photonA single "packet" or quantumThe smallest indivisible unit of something — here, of light energy. Plural: quanta. Light comes in discrete energy packets, not a continuous stream. of light. Light behaves both as a wave and as a stream of these particles..

Background — why short wavelength means high energy A photon's energy is E = hc/λ: h is Planck's constant, c the speed of light, λ the wavelength. Energy is inversely proportional to λ, so halving the wavelength doubles the photon energy. This is why UV (short λ) damages tissue and X-rays ionise, while infrared (long λ) mainly just heats.

What the eye does with each band

The eye is a stack of filters. Going front to back:

  • The corneaThe clear, dome-shaped front window of the eye. It does most of the eye's focusing and is the first tissue light meets. and scleraThe tough white outer coat of the eyeball ("the white of the eye"). Opaque and protective. absorb essentially all the very short UV (UV-B, UV-C) and the long IR (IR-B, IR-C).
  • UV-A that gets through is then strongly absorbed by the crystalline lensThe eye's adjustable internal lens, sitting just behind the iris. It fine-tunes focus and, usefully, soaks up UV-A — protecting the retina behind it..
  • Wavelengths 400–1400 nm (visible light + near infrared) pass through the ocular mediaThe transparent structures light crosses to reach the retina: cornea, aqueous humour, lens, and vitreous gel. and reach the retinaThe light-sensitive neural lining at the back of the eye — the "film" of the camera. It converts light into nerve signals..

Visible wavelengths stimulate the photoreceptorsRetinal cells that convert light into electrical signals — rods (dim light, no colour) and cones (bright light, colour). and give the sensation of light. Near infrared is not "seen" but is focused onto the retina by the same refracting surfaces, so it can cause thermal injury — classically eclipse burns (solar retinopathy).

Exam Memorise the seven bands and their cut-offs (200/280/315/400/780/1400/3000/10 000 nm), and the absorption map: cornea+sclera → UV-B/C and IR-B/C; lens → UV-A; 400–1400 nm → retina. The visible window (400–780 nm) and the "reaches-retina" window (400–1400 nm) are different numbers — examiners exploit the gap.
Quick check

Which ocular structure is the principal absorber of UV-A, and why does that matter clinically?

The crystalline lens. Because the lens shields the retina from UV-A, removing it (cataract surgery → aphakiaThe state of having no crystalline lens, e.g. after cataract removal without an implant.) lets UV-A reach the retina — relevant to perceived colour shifts and to designing UV-filtering lens implants.

2 · Colour vision

An object's colour is set by which wavelengths it emits or reflects. White light is a mixture of all visible wavelengths. The eye encodes colour with three cone populations, each tuned to a different region of the spectrum:

ConeSensitive toPeak (Elkington Fig 1.2)
Short (S) — "blue"short λ~440 nm
Middle (M) — "green"middle λ~535 nm
Long (L) — "red"long λ~560 nm
Background — the "red" cone isn't red The L-cone peaks around 560 nm, which is yellow-green, not red. Colour isn't read off one cone; the brain compares the ratio of signals across all three. A pure red is signalled by the L cone responding more than the M cone — not by a cone that peaks at red. This trips people up constantly.

Congenital colour defects

A congenital defect arises when a cone pigment is absent or its peak is shifted. The naming is systematic — learn the two suffixes:

Cone affectedAbsent (-anopia)Shifted (-anomaly)
Red (L)protanopiaprotanomaly
Green (M)deuteranopiadeuteranomaly
Blue (S)tritanopiatritanomaly

Genetics: the X chromosome carries the red and green pigment genes; chromosome 7 carries the blue pigment gene. Because red/green genes are X-linked, defects are far commoner in men.

  • Red/green defect: 8% of men, 0.5% of women.
  • Commonest single type: deuteranomaly — 5% of men, 0.3% of women.
  • Tritan (blue) defects are rare.
Background — why "prot-/deuter-/trit-" map to red/green/blue The prefixes are Greek ordinals: protos = first (the L/red system), deuteros = second (M/green), tritos = third (S/blue). So prot = red, deuter = green, trit = blue — first-second-third = red-green-blue. A reliable mnemonic for a fact that's otherwise easy to scramble.

Acquired colour defects

Congenital defects hit a particular part of the spectrum. Acquired defects spread across the spectrum but can be worse in some regions. The clinically examined patterns:

  • Acquired optic nerve disease → red-green defect.
  • Exception: glaucoma and autosomal dominant optic neuropathy initially cause a predominantly blue-yellow deficit. (Indeed, glaucomatous field loss is detected earlier by perimetry using a blue stimulus on a yellow background.)
  • Acquired retinal disease → blue-yellow defect.
  • Exception: cone dystrophy and Stargardt's disease cause a predominantly red-green defect.
Exam The classic two-line rule with its two exceptions is high-yield: optic nerve = red-green (but glaucoma/ADOA = blue-yellow); retina = blue-yellow (but cone dystrophy/Stargardt = red-green). Köllner's rule, with both exceptions, is a favourite.
Quick check

A man has the commonest congenital colour defect. Name it, state which cone is affected and how, and give its rough prevalence.

Deuteranomaly — a shift (not absence) of the green (M) cone's sensitivity; ~5% of men (0.3% of women).

3 · Testing colour vision

By convention, colour tests are designed for illumination equivalent to afternoon daylight in the northern hemisphere.

Farnsworth–Munsell (FM) 100 hue test

The most comprehensive method. 84 coloured discs (numbered on the undersurface), divided into four groups of 21, each group covering a portion of the spectrum. The discs differ only in hue — brightness and saturationHow pure or intense a colour is. A highly saturated red is vivid; a desaturated red is washed-out/pinkish. Here all discs are matched for saturation so only hue varies. are equal — so the only cue is colour. The subject arranges each group in order of closest colour match between fixed reference discs at the ends; the error pattern reveals the type of defect.

D-15 test

Uses colours from across the spectrum that must be ordered from a single reference cap. It does not detect mild defects, but anyone who passes is unlikely to have practically significant hue-discrimination problems — a quick screen.

Ishihara pseudoisochromatic plates

Specifically screen for congenital red-green defects (the commonest). Each plate is a field of dots; numbers (or wavy lines, for the illiterate) are embedded as dots of a different colour but the same brightness. The figure can be picked out only by colour, not by contrast — so a colour-blind person sees a random pattern or reads the wrong figure.

Background — "pseudoisochromatic" "Iso-chromatic" = same colour; "pseudo-isochromatic" = falsely the same colour. The dots are deliberately matched in lightness so that, to a colour-defective eye, figure and background look identical — there's no brightness edge to give the answer away.

Lanthony New Colour Test

Tests hue discrimination and is usable by children.

Quick check

Why can an Ishihara plate only be read using colour information — what design feature enforces this?

The figure dots and background dots are matched for brightness/contrast, so there's no luminance edge. The number is defined purely by a hue difference, which a red-green-defective eye cannot resolve.

4 · Ultraviolet light & retinal hazard

The photoreceptors are actually sensitive down into the near UV (400–350 nm, UV-A) — but normally the lens absorbs these before they arrive. Remove or replace the lens without a UV filter and that light gets through:

  • In aphakicNo crystalline lens present (e.g. after cataract removal with no implant). or pseudophakicA crystalline lens that has been replaced by an artificial intraocular lensAn implanted artificial lens (IOL) placed inside the eye, usually after cataract removal. implant. eyes without a UV filter, UV-A reaches the retina and is perceived as blue/violet. Newly aphakic patients classically say "everything looks bluer."
  • More worrying: wavelengths from 350 nm (UV) up to 441 nm (visible) appear to be the most dangerous for retinal damage under ordinary environmental light. So modern implant lenses incorporate a UV-A-absorbing substance to protect the retina.

Instrument light is also a hazard. Prolonged high-intensity exposure from the indirect ophthalmoscope, the intraocular light pipe (used in vitreoretinal surgery), and the operating microscope can damage the retina — sometimes one already compromised. Some instruments add yellow filters to cut the most damaging short wavelengths.

Quick check

Why do UV-filtering intraocular lenses exist, and what band are they targeting?

Removing the natural lens removes the eye's UV-A shield. IOLs with a UV-A absorber re-create that protection, blocking roughly the 350–441 nm band thought most hazardous to the retina.

5 · Fluorescence & fluorescein angiography

FluorescenceThe property of a molecule to absorb light at a short wavelength and almost instantly re-emit light at a longer (lower-energy) wavelength. The energy difference is lost as heat/molecular motion. is the ability of a molecule to absorb short-wavelength light and spontaneously emit longer-wavelength light. The dye fluorescein sodium (orange) is excited by blue light (465–490 nm) and emits yellow-green light (520–530 nm).

Background — why the emitted light is always longer An absorbed photon excites the molecule; some of that energy dissipates internally before a photon is re-emitted, so the emitted photon carries less energy — and lower energy means longer wavelength (recall E = hc/λ). The shift to longer wavelength is called the Stokes shift, and it's what makes the emitted glow separable from the exciting light.

Fundus fluorescein angiography (FFA)

FFA images the retinal and choroidal circulation by photographing fluorescein as it passes through the vessels after being injected systemically. The optical trick is two matched filters:

  • White camera-flash light passes a blue "excitation" filter → only blue light (matching fluorescein's absorption) hits the fundus.
  • At the fundus: most blue is absorbed, some reflects unchanged, and some is converted to yellow-green by fluorescence.
  • Light leaving the eye passes a yellow-green "barrier" filter, which blocks the reflected blue and lets only the yellow-green fluorescent light reach the film — so the image shows only the dye, outlining vessels and any leakage.

PseudofluorescenceA false "fluorescent" signal on FFA caused not by the dye but by reflected light leaking through. happens when the excitation and barrier filters overlap in transmission: reflected wavelengths at the green end of blue slip through the barrier filter and masquerade as fluorescence.

Other fluorescein uses: staining ocular-surface (epithelial) defects, anterior-segment angiography, measuring aqueous humour production and outflow, and — in light microscopy — localising tissue components via fluorescein bound to specific immunoglobulinAn antibody protein. Tagging an antibody with fluorescein lets you light up wherever that antibody binds — the basis of immunofluorescence. (immunofluorescence).

Exam Lock in the fluorescein numbers: excited by blue 465–490 nm, emits yellow-green 520–530 nm. And the principle: excitation filter ≈ absorption spectrum; barrier filter ≈ emission spectrum; filter overlap → pseudofluorescence.

6 · Indocyanine green (ICG)

ICG is a fluorescent dye working in the infrared: it absorbs 805 nm and emits 835 nm. Crucially, the retinal pigment epitheliumThe RPE — a single layer of pigmented cells just behind the photoreceptors. Its melanin absorbs visible light, which on FFA can mask the choroid behind it. does not absorb these IR wavelengths, so ICG (given intravenously) lets you image the choroidalThe choroid is the vascular layer between retina and sclera that supplies the outer retina with blood. Hard to see on FFA because the RPE screens it; ICG sees through. circulation that fluorescein struggles to show.

ICG is far less efficient: only about 4% of absorbed 805 nm light is re-emitted at 835 nm, versus the much higher fluorescence of fluorescein. Per Elkington's era it was "not yet in general clinical use," but it was shown to reveal occult choroidal neovascularisationCNV — abnormal new blood vessels growing from the choroid, e.g. in wet age-related macular degeneration. "Occult" CNV is ill-defined/hidden on fluorescein but can be delineated by ICG. invisible on fluorescein, and to photosensitise vascular lesions for diode-laser photocoagulation.

Quick check

Why is ICG better than fluorescein for imaging the choroid?

ICG works in the infrared (805/835 nm), which the RPE does not absorb. The RPE therefore does not screen the choroidal circulation from view, so ICG images it directly — and can show occult choroidal neovascularisation.

7 · The wave nature of light

Within a uniform medium (and meeting no obstacle or interface), light travels in straight lines. We draw it as a rayA straight, arrowed line representing the path of light. By tradition, optical diagrams show rays travelling left-to-right across the page.. But ray diagrams don't explain everything light does, so for some phenomena we model light as a wave.

Three equivalent pictures of light leaving a point source: (a) straight rays radiating outward; (b) each ray as an oscillating wave; (c) wavefrontsSurfaces (here, concentric circles/spheres) joining all points of a wave that are at the same phase — drawn through the crests. They expand outward from the source like ripples from a stone dropped in water. — concentric circles through the crests, exactly like ripples spreading from a stone dropped in still water.

Wave motion is energy (a disturbance) travelling through a medium; the medium itself doesn't move along. Its particles vibrate at right angles to the direction of travel — a transverseA wave in which the oscillation is perpendicular to the direction the wave travels (e.g. a flicked rope). Light is transverse — which is exactly why it can be polarised (see §13). Sound, by contrast, is longitudinal. wave. Picture a ribbon tied to a rope you flick: the wave runs along the rope, but the ribbon only bobs up and down.

Key definitions:

  • Wavelength (λ) — the distance between two matching points of the wave (e.g. crest to crest).
  • Cycle — one complete oscillation; it occupies exactly one wavelength.
  • Amplitude (A) — the maximum displacement from the baseline. (Brightness/intensity scales with amplitude.)
  • PhaseWhere you are within a cycle, expressed as a fraction of the cycle or as an angle (a full cycle = 360°). Two waves "in step" are in phase; "out of step" means a phase difference. — any portion of a cycle.
  • Phase difference — for two waves of equal wavelength travelling together but out of step, the fraction of a cycle by which one leads. A quarter-wavelength lead = 90° (a full cycle being 360°).

Light made of waves exactly in step is coherent; waves out of phase are incoherent. (Laser light is the classic coherent source.)

Quick check

Light is a transverse wave. Which later property does that fact directly enable?

Polarisation. Only transverse waves have an oscillation plane that can be selectively transmitted or blocked (§13). Longitudinal waves like sound cannot be polarised.

8 · Interference

When two light waves travel the same path, they add together (algebraically — crest plus crest, crest plus trough), a process called superpositionThe principle that when waves overlap, the resulting displacement at each point is the sum of the individual displacements. Add the heights; positives and negatives can reinforce or cancel.. The outcome depends on their phase:

  • Constructive interference — in phase: the waves sum to a larger wave.
  • Destructive interference — out of phase by half a cycle and equal amplitude: they cancel completely.
  • Phase differences between these give an intermediate amplitude and phase.

Where interference matters in the eye and clinic

  • Corneal transparency. The collagen bundles (fibrils) of the corneal stromaThe thick, collagen-rich middle layer that makes up most of the cornea's bulk. are spaced so regularly that light scattered sideways by them is wiped out by destructive interference — which is a large part of why the cornea is clear rather than milky.
  • Anti-reflection (low-reflection) lens coatings. A thin transparent film of the right thickness is applied to a lens. Light reflecting off the film's front surface and light reflecting off its back surface are half a cycle apart and destructively interfere — cancelling the reflection so more light is transmitted.
Exam Two favourite "interference in vivo/instruments" facts: regular corneal collagen spacing → destructive interference → transparency, and thin-film anti-reflection coatings work by destructive interference.

9 · Diffraction & the Airy disc

DiffractionThe spreading/bending of waves as they pass the edge of an obstacle or a narrow opening. The edge behaves as a new source of secondary wavelets that are out of phase with the original wave. is what happens when a wavefront meets a narrow opening or the edge of an obstruction: the wave spreads out on the far side, as if the edge were a fresh source of secondary wavefronts (out of phase with the primary ones).

Behind a straight edge, the directly-lit zone is slightly dimmed by interference between primary and secondary waves, and the geometric "shadow" zone receives a faint glow from secondary waves alone. Through a circular aperture, light forms a circular diffraction pattern: a bright central disc ringed by alternating dark and light rings. That central bright disc is the Airy discThe bright central blob of the diffraction pattern from a circular aperture — the smallest "spot" a perfect lens can form from a point source. Its size sets the ultimate resolution limit of any optical system, including the eye..

Diffraction is worst with small apertures and occurs in every optical system — lenses, instruments and the eye. The trade-offs:

  • In lenses and instruments, diffraction at the apertures used is negligible compared with their other faults (aberrationsImperfections in an optical system that blur or distort the image, separate from diffraction — e.g. spherical and chromatic aberration. Covered in Chapter 8.).
  • In the eye, diffraction is the main cause of image imperfection when the pupilThe central aperture of the iris through which light enters the eye; it widens in dim light and constricts in bright light. is small. A larger pupil reduces diffraction — but that gain is outweighed by the increasing aberrations of a wide-open eye.

Diffraction is also harnessed deliberately in the design of some diffractive multifocal intraocular lenses.

Quick check

For the eye, is a very small pupil or a very large pupil better for image quality — and what limits each?

Neither extreme is ideal. A small pupil is limited by diffraction; a large pupil is limited by aberrations. Optimal optical quality sits at an intermediate pupil size (around 2–3 mm).

10 · Resolution & resolving power

The limit of resolution (or resolving power) is the smallest angle of separation, w, between two points that still lets a system form two distinguishable images. Because each point is imaged as an Airy disc, the limit is reached when the two discs are so close that the centre of one falls on the first dark ring of the other.

Background — this is the Rayleigh criterion The "centre of one Airy disc on the first dark ring of the other" rule is the Rayleigh criterion for resolution. It explains why a small pupil (or any small aperture) gives big Airy discs and therefore poor resolution: less detail can be separated.

11 · Testing visual acuity

Clinically we want to measure the eye's resolving power — its visual acuity. The right test depends on the patient's age and ability.

Infants and pre-verbal children

Examine babies when alert and not hungry. Good fixation is CSM — central, steady and maintained. The best target is a face (the mother's), a toy, or a TV cartoon. Signs of poor acuity: strong eye preference (objecting when the good eye is covered), squintStrabismus — misalignment of the eyes., nystagmusInvoluntary rhythmic to-and-fro eye movements; here a sign that vision is poor., roving gaze and eye-poking.

  • Visually directed reaching develops at 2–5 months; if vision is poor, movements look exploratory.
  • A child of ≥15 months picking up a tiny "hundreds-and-thousands" sweet suggests near acuity ~6/24 or better and no serious defect — though good near acuity can still coexist with high myopiaShort-sightedness: the eye focuses distant objects in front of the retina, so far things blur but near things may be seen clearly..
  • Catford drum — a white cylinder of black dots of increasing size (acuities 6/6 to 2/60 at 60 cm), masked so one oscillating spot shows; following the spot indicates it's seen. Overestimates acuity (target moves; short working distance).
  • STYCAR rolling balls — ten white polystyrene spheres (3.5 mm to 6 cm) rolled across a contrasting floor 3 m away; pursuit movements show they're seen. (Worth's ivory ball test is similar.)
  • Preferential looking — a striped target (a black/white square-wave grating) is shown beside a plain grey target of equal size and average luminance; infants prefer to look at pattern, and better vision detects a finer grating.
  • Visual evoked potentialsVEPs — electrical responses recorded over the occipital (visual) cortex when the eye is stimulated, using a reversing grating or chequerboard. An objective, non-behavioural acuity measure..
Background — "square-wave" vs "sine-wave" grating A square-wave grating has sharp-edged black and white stripes (used for acuity/preferential looking and VEPs). A sine-wave grating fades gradually between light and dark with blurred edges (used for contrast sensitivity, §12). Same idea — stripes — but the edge profile differs.

Matching and naming (older children and adults)

An optotypeA standardised symbol (letter, number or picture) whose correct identification corresponds to a defined level of visual acuity. Always black on a white background to maximise contrast. is a symbol calibrated to an acuity level. Children too young to name letters can match a shown letter to a key card (examiner at 6 m or 3 m):

  • Kay's pictures / Cardiff cards — picture optotypes, usable from ~18–24 months.
  • STYCAR letters — H, O, T, V, X (the letters children recognise first), up to ~3 years.
  • Sheridan–Gardiner — seven letters, adding U and A.

The Snellen chart

The most-used test. Its theory: the smallest object the eye can resolve subtends, at the eye's nodal pointAn optical reference point inside the eye through which a ray passes without changing direction. The angle a target subtends at the nodal point equals the angle its image subtends on the retina. (Defined fully later in optics.), the same angle as one cone — about one minute of arc.

Background — minutes and seconds of arc Angles get subdivided like time: 1 degree = 60 minutes of arc ('); 1 minute = 60 seconds of arc ("). So 1' = 1/60° ≈ 0.0167°, and 1" is 1/3600 of a degree — tiny. Acuity is built on arcminutes; hyperacuity and stereoacuity (below) are measured in arcseconds.

Each Snellen letter is built on a 5 × 5 grid: the whole letter subtends 5' and each limb/gap subtends 1' at the test distance. Each row is labelled with the distance (in metres) at which a normal eye should read it.

So Snellen acuity is a fraction test distance / distance a normal eye reads that row. Read normally at 6 m (20 feet):

  • 6/6 (20/20) = normal; the numerator is the viewing distance.
  • 6/12 (20/40) = at 6 m you only manage the row a normal eye reads at 12 m.
  • Young adults often reach 6/4 (better than "normal").

Children sometimes manage Snellen before age 5, and letter-matching before 4.

LogMAR (e.g. Bailey–Lovie)

LogMAR = LOGarithm of the Minimum Angle of Resolution. These charts are more precise than Snellen because the letter size and spacing step regularly, with the same number of letters on every line, so results are comparable at any test distance.

Background — reading the logMAR scale MAR = the smallest detail resolved, in arcminutes; logMAR = log₁₀(MAR). At 6/6 the detail is 1' so logMAR = log₁₀(1) = 0.00. At 6/60 the detail is 10' so logMAR = log₁₀(10) = 1.00. Higher logMAR = worse vision (the opposite intuition to Snellen). Each Bailey–Lovie line is 0.1 log units; each letter is worth 0.02.

Vernier acuity (hyperacuity)

Vernier acuityThe ability to detect a tiny sideways misalignment (offset) between two line segments — like reading a vernier scale. Tested with an offset square-wave grating. is the smallest detectable offset of a line. We can spot an offset of just 3–5 seconds of arc — finer than the Snellen limit, hence hyperacuity.

Quick check

A patient reads at 6 m only the line a normal eye reads at 18 m. Give their Snellen and logMAR acuity.

Snellen 6/18. logMAR = log₁₀(18 ÷ 6) = log₁₀(3) ≈ 0.48.

12 · Near, potential, contrast & glare testing

Near visual acuity

Tested at 25–33 cm, using unrelated words or short passages. The British N system is based on the printers' pointA typesetting unit for type size. One point = 1/72 inch. It originally specified the height of the metal block a letter was cast on. system: 1 point = 1/72 inch, and N5 letters are cast on blocks 5/72 inch high. Times Roman is the standard font (printed size depends on font). Jaeger ("J") sizes are a poorer alternative because they don't follow a logical size progression.

Potential visual acuity

Used to predict how well an eye could see when the macula can't be examined (e.g. behind a cataractA clouding of the crystalline lens that scatters and blocks light, reducing vision. The commonest treatable cause of visual loss worldwide.). Good potential acuity suggests surgery will help.

  • Pinhole test — the simplest.
  • Blue field entoptic phenomenonAn "entoptic" phenomenon is something you see that arises inside your own eye. Under diffuse blue light you can see your own moving white dots. — moving white dots seen under diffuse blue light, thought to be light passing white blood cells in the perifoveal capillaries; their presence implies macular function is grossly intact.
  • Interferometers — project laser light from two sources onto the retina; where they meet, interference produces a sine-wave grating the patient can report if the macula works.
  • Potential acuity meter (PAM) — projects a letter chart onto the retina through a small aperture.

Contrast sensitivity

Acuity charts use high-contrast black-on-white and don't capture the ability to see low-contrast objects (like faces). In cataract, glaucoma and optic neuritisInflammation of the optic nerve, classically reducing vision and colour perception, often with pain on eye movement., acuity can be near-normal while contrast sensitivity is markedly reduced.

Measured with a sine-wave grating (gradual light↔dark transitions, blurred edges). Narrower stripes = higher spatial frequencyHow tightly packed the stripes are, usually in cycles per degree. High spatial frequency = fine stripes; low = broad stripes.. Plotting spatial frequency against the lowest detectable contrast gives the contrast sensitivity curve; both very low and very high spatial frequencies need higher contrast to be seen (the curve peaks in the middle).

  • Pelli–Robson chart — letters of decreasing contrast.
  • VISTECH chart — 40 circles of differing sine-wave gratings and contrasts; the subject reports each grating's orientation.

Glare testing

Glare is scattered light that reduces visual function — often the main complaint in corneal oedema or scarring, cataract, or posterior capsule opacificationClouding of the thin lens capsule left behind after cataract surgery — a common, easily treated late cause of glare and blur.. Its impact depends on the glare source's position and intensity and on the media's light-scattering. Glare testing means measuring visual function (acuity, contrast sensitivity, colour) in the presence of a glare source.

Exam Acuity ≠ visual function. The teaching point examiners want: cataract/glaucoma/optic neuritis can give near-normal Snellen acuity but reduced contrast sensitivity. Also: acuity uses square-wave gratings; contrast sensitivity uses sine-wave gratings.
Quick check

How tall is the metal block for an N5 near-acuity letter, and which font is the N-system standard?

5/72 inch (since 1 point = 1/72 inch and N5 = 5 points). The standard font is Times Roman.

13 · Polarisation, birefringence & dichroism

In ordinary light, the planes in which the rays vibrate are oriented randomly. In polarisedLight whose waves all vibrate in a single plane, rather than in random orientations. Possible only because light is a transverse wave. light, the wave motions all lie parallel to one another — one shared plane.

Producing polarised light

  • By a polarising substance. Materials such as calcite crystals transmit only rays vibrating in one plane, so only a fraction of the light passes and what emerges is polarised. A polariser reduces intensity but not spectral composition (colour).
  • By reflection. Light reflected from a flat surface (e.g. water) is polarised when the angle of incidence equals that surface's polarising angleThe specific angle of incidence at which reflected light becomes fully plane-polarised. It depends on the surface's refractive index (the Brewster angle). At other angles the reflection is only partly polarised., which depends on the surface's refractive indexA number describing how much a material slows and bends light (covered in later optics chapters). Higher index = light travels slower and bends more.. At other angles the reflection is partly polarised. The reflected light's plane of polarisation lies parallel to the surface — and since everyday glare-surfaces (sea, wet roads) are horizontal, the glare is horizontally polarised.

Polarised sunglasses exploit this: they transmit only vertical polarisation, blocking the horizontally-polarised reflected glare from water and roads.

Birefringence

Birefringence"Double refraction." A material with two refractive indices, which splits unpolarised light into two polarised beams travelling in different directions.: some materials transmit waves vibrating parallel to their molecular structure but selectively slow and redirect waves vibrating perpendicular to it. Quartz is birefringent — it splits incident unpolarised light into two polarised beams going different ways, so it has two refractive indices.

Dichroism

DichroismA property where a material absorbs (rather than redirects) the light waves not aligned with its structure, so only one polarised beam emerges — weakened.: dichroic materials absorb the waves not aligned with their structure, so a single, much-weakened polarised beam emerges. Tourmaline and Polaroid (fine iodine + quinine sulphate crystals embedded in plastic) are dichroic; Polaroid is the usual sunglass material.

Background — birefringence vs dichroism in one line Birefringence splits and redirects into two beams (two refractive indices, no absorption); dichroism absorbs one component, leaving one weakened beam. Different mechanism, both yield polarised light.

Other ophthalmic uses of polarised light: dissociating the two eyes to assess binocular vision (e.g. the polarised Titmus stereotest, §14); producing Haidinger's brushes in pleoptics; and examining manufactured lenses for internal stress.

Quick check

Why are polarised sunglasses oriented to block horizontal polarisation, and what's the structural difference between quartz and Polaroid in handling polarisation?

Glare reflected off horizontal surfaces (sea, wet roads) is horizontally polarised, so blocking that plane removes the glare. Quartz is birefringent (splits light into two redirected polarised beams; two refractive indices); Polaroid is dichroic (absorbs the unwanted plane, leaving one weakened beam).

14 · Stereoscopic vision

Stereopsis3-D depth perception generated by the brain fusing the two eyes' slightly different views of the same scene. is the ability to fuse the two eyes' slightly different (disparate) images — which stimulate disparate retinal points within Panum's fusional areasSmall regions around corresponding retinal points within which slightly disparate images can still be fused into one (with depth) rather than seen double. — into a single percept with depth. It's graded by the smallest horizontal image disparity that still evokes depth, measured in seconds of arc.

  • Normal stereoacuity ≈ 60 seconds of arc or better.
  • Very good vision: better than 15" (the finest the Frisby offers; range 600–15").
  • Best where images land on the macula (highest resolving power) — so good stereoacuity is a product of central single binocular vision.
  • Better than 250" excludes significant amblyopia"Lazy eye" — reduced vision in an eye that is structurally normal, due to abnormal visual development in childhood (e.g. from squint or unequal refractive error).; worse than 250" may indicate it.

Clinical stereoacuity tests (UK common: Titmus, TNO, Frisby, Lang)

  • Titmus (includes the Wirt fly) — uses vectographsA pair of images printed so that the light from each is plane-polarised at right angles; viewed through a polarising visor, each eye sees only its own image, creating disparity.. The Wirt fly is the largest target; also graded animals and circles (one stands forward). Viewed at 40 cm; range ~3000–40".
  • Frisby — three clear plastic plates of different thickness; four panels of random shapes, one hiding a circle printed on the back surface. Purely three-dimensional, no glasses needed. At 40 cm the plates give 340/170/55"; adjusting distance extends the range to 600–15".
  • TNO — random-dot anaglyphsA stereogram with the two views printed in red and green on white; through red-green glasses each eye sees only one view (as black), and the brain fuses them. viewed with red-green glasses; range 480–15".
  • Lang — fine vertical lines seen alternately by each eye through built-in cylindrical lens elements; random-dot displacement gives disparity 1200–550". Held parallel to the face (tilting gives monocular clues); normal reading distance.
Exam Worth knowing: normal stereoacuity ~60"; the 250" amblyopia cut-off; that the Frisby and Lang need no special glasses (Frisby = real depth between plate surfaces; Lang = built-in cylindrical lenses), whereas Titmus uses polarised glasses and TNO uses red-green glasses.
Quick check

Which two of the four common stereotests require no coloured/polarising glasses, and what makes each work?

Frisby (a true physical depth difference — the hidden circle is printed on the plate's back surface) and Lang (built-in cylindrical lens elements feed each eye a different image). Titmus needs polarised glasses; TNO needs red-green glasses.

15 · Measuring light: radiometry & photometry

Two parallel systems, which is why the units feel like a zoo:

  • RadiometryPhysical measurement of radiant energy across the whole electromagnetic spectrum, as an absolute value — regardless of whether the eye can see it. — measures the raw physical energy (any wavelength).
  • PhotometryMeasurement weighted by the eye's spectral sensitivity — i.e. how much the light contributes to the sensation of brightness. More commonly used in visual science. — measures light as the eye perceives it.

The two are linked by luminous efficiencyA per-wavelength conversion factor reflecting how strongly the eye responds to that wavelength. It links a radiometric (physical) unit to its photometric (perceived) equivalent.. The eye's peak photopic sensitivity is at 555 nm (yellow-green), where 1 watt of monochromatic light ≈ 685 lumens (maximum luminous efficiency). Sensitivity falls towards both ends of the spectrum, dropping to zero outside ~400–700 nm. For mixed (polychromatic) light, you sum the photometric equivalents of the component wavelengths.

Uncertain — a figure to verify Elkington gives the luminous efficacy peak as 685 lm/W. The modern SI value (defining the candela) is 683 lm/W at 555 nm for photopic vision. The difference is small and historical; I'd revise from 685 to 683 for the modern definition, but check your current source for which the exam expects.
Background — photopic vs scotopic (not in this chapter, but high-yield) 555 nm is the photopic (cone, bright-light) peak. Under dark (scotopic, rod) conditions the peak shifts to ~507 nm (blue-green) — the Purkinje shift, why reds look black and blues relatively brighter at dusk. Elkington gives only the photopic figure here; the scotopic value is a settled add-on worth knowing.

Background — what's a steradian (solid angle)? A steradianThe unit of solid angle (sr) — the 3-D analogue of the radian. It's the cone-shaped angle at a sphere's centre that cuts out a patch of area r² on the sphere's surface. measures a cone of directions rather than a flat angle. Because a whole sphere's surface is 4πr², a full sphere contains 4π steradians. So a point source of 1 candela (1 lm/sr) emitting in all directions puts out 4π ≈ 12.57 lumens total. (For the calculator-free exam, remember 4π ≈ 12.57.)
Background — candle → candela The old unit was the candle (a standard wax candle). The modern candela was defined via a black-body radiator at the freezing point of platinum, whose luminous intensity per cm² is 60 candelas — being 60× brighter than the standard candle. The key takeaway: candela = lumens per steradian, a measure of intensity in one direction, not total output.

How illumination of a surface behaves

Surface illumination falls off with distance and with the angle of the light:

  • Inverse-square law — illumination ∝ 1/d² (double the distance → a quarter the illumination).
  • Cosine law — illumination ∝ cos i (i = angle of incidence; light striking at a slant is spread over more area).

Combined: E = (I · cos i) / d², where E = illumination, I = luminous intensity, i = angle of incidence, d = source-to-surface distance.

Diffusers and a couple of special units

A uniformly diffusing surface reflects light equally in all directions; a perfect diffuser also reflects all incident light. Each point on it acts as a point source. Luminance = luminous intensity per unit projected area in a given direction (cd/m²); its radiometric twin is radiance (W/sr/m²). Because measuring in candelas is awkward, luminance is often compared with a uniform diffuser emitting 1 lumen per unit area:

  • Apostilb (asb)A unit of luminance: the luminance of a uniform diffuser emitting 1 lumen per square metre. Equivalently, 1 asb = 1/π cd/m². You'll meet it again in perimetry. — 1 lm/m² of diffuse emission = 1 apostilb = 1/π cd/m².
  • TrolandA unit of retinal illumination: the retinal illumination when a surface of luminance 1 cd/m² is viewed through a 1 mm² entrance pupil (after correcting for the Stiles–Crawford effect). It accounts for how much light actually reaches the retina. — a measure of retinal illumination (surface luminance 1 cd/m² through a 1 mm² pupil), corrected for the Stiles–Crawford effectLight entering through the pupil centre stimulates cones more effectively than light entering near the pupil edge — so where the light enters matters, not just how much..
Exam Pin down the photometric units and their SI names: flux = lumen; intensity = candela (lm/sr); illuminance = lux (lm/m²); luminance = cd/m². And three derivable facts: a 1 cd point source emits 4π lm; 1 apostilb = 1/π cd/m²; illumination follows E = I cos i / d².
Quick check

A point source has luminous intensity 1 candela. (a) What total luminous flux does it emit? (b) If you double your distance from it, how does the illumination on your page change?

(a) 4π ≈ 12.57 lumens (1 cd = 1 lm/sr × 4π sr). (b) It falls to one-quarter (inverse-square law).

16 · Automated perimetry

PerimetryMapping the visual field by testing how dim a light spot the patient can detect at many positions — i.e. measuring the light sensitivity of corresponding retinal points. measures retinal light sensitivity point-by-point: the patient detects light stimuli of varying intensity at corresponding positions in the visual field. Practical set-up: a standard background luminance of 31.5 apostilbs; the eye centred in the bowl (hemisphere); near correction worn; steady fixation; spots projected onto the inner surface.

Stimulus intensity ranges 0.8–10 000 asb. This huge range is handled on a logarithmic scale in decibels (dB), where 1 log unit = 10 dB (a dim spot = many dB of attenuation; the brightest = 0 dB). Elkington states the 0.8–10 000 asb range corresponds to 51 dB.

Uncertain — the arithmetic doesn't quite close By the stated rule (10 dB per log unit), 0.8 → 10 000 asb is a ratio of 12 500 ≈ 4.1 log units ≈ ~41 dB, not 51 dB. Getting 51 dB would need the dimmest stimulus near 0.08 asb (a possible typographic slip in the source). I've reported Elkington's figures faithfully but flag the mismatch — verify the exact numbers against a current perimetry reference before quoting them.
Background — why decibels at all? The eye's detectable intensity range is enormous, so a linear scale is unwieldy. A decibel here expresses attenuation of the maximum stimulus: higher dB = the spot was dimmer when the patient still saw it = better sensitivity. It's the same log-scale logic as sound decibels.
Quick check

On a perimeter, does a higher decibel value at a test point mean better or worse sensitivity, and why?

Better. dB measures how much the maximum stimulus was dimmed and still seen — a higher dB means the patient detected a dimmer spot, i.e. greater retinal sensitivity at that point.
